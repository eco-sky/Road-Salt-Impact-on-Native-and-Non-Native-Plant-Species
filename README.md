# **Dataset for Assessment of Road Salt Impact on Native and Non-native Plant Species**

## **Dataset Abstract**

Plant Growth Data sheet contains experimental results for Western Yarrow and White Dutch Clover, including average height (cm), germination, growth rate (height/day), and additional observations. Data are organized by pot ID, species, treatment, road salt concentration, and day of measurement, allowing analysis of growth and germination responses across treatments and over time.

Growth and Germination sheet contains mean values and standard deviations for germination and growth rate (height per day) across control, low, medium, and high salt treatments for Western Yarrow and White Dutch Clover, including associated standard error and 95% confidence intervals.

Germination Measurements sheet contains germination data for each replicate, including pot ID, species, treatment, road salt concentration, and the day of first germination used to calculate mean germination timing across treatments.
Remaining sheets outputs from statistical tests and descriptive statistics for plant growth and germination (N, mean, SD, min, max, quartiles) for all species and separately for Western Yarrow and White Dutch Clover. Treatment differences were assessed using Kruskal-Wallis and Wilcoxon tests.


R code and data analysis for an environmental capstone project assessing the impact of road salt on native and non-native plant species. This project examines how increasing salinity influences germination and growth of Western Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) and White Dutch Clover (Trifolium repens) through controlled experiments and field surveys of urban stream ecosystems in Metro Vancouver. The repository includes data processing, statistical analysis, and visualization workflows in R, along with experimental and field datasets (plant growth, germination, and water quality measurements) and outputs from non-parametric statistical tests (Kruskal-Wallis and Wilcoxon).

## **Capstone Project Abstract**

Across urban British Columbia (BC), winter road salt use increases soil and freshwater salinity, with potential impacts on plant growth and community composition. Salt stress can reduce native plant performance while favouring salt-tolerant non-native species, potentially altering ecosystem balance, yet their relative responses remain poorly understood. This study conducted a plant experiment and examined how increasing salt concentrations influence germination and growth of native Western Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) and non-native White Dutch Clover (Trifolium repens), with the expectation that salinity would reduce native plant growth more strongly. To contextualize these results, a field survey of plant cover and water quality was conducted across six urban streams in Metro Vancouver, spanning historically low and high conductivity conditions. Germination was rapid in both species (99% within 4 to 6 days), with White Dutch Clover germinating faster (4.4 ± 0.1 days vs 5.2 ± 0.1 days) and exhibiting higher growth under control conditions (0.19 ± 0.01 cm/day compared to 0.097 ± 0.007 cm/day). High salinity delayed germination in White Dutch Clover but not Western Yarrow, with low and high salinity reduced growth in both species and medium salinity increased growth only in White Dutch Clover. Results from the field survey showed no consistent relationship between electrical conductivity (EC) and overall biodiversity, although high-EC streams had greater species evenness. However, low-EC streams had significantly higher total and native vegetation cover compared to high-EC streams. Overall, these findings suggest that moderate salinity may favour non native species, supporting the hypothesis that road salt exposure can shift plant community composition in urban ecosystems.
